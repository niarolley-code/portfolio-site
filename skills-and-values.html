Skills & Values

The Lens I Operate Through

Everything I build, write, organize, and teach is filtered through a specific point of view — one I didn’t choose so much as inherit and then learned to wield intentionally.

As a Black woman, I sit at the center of intersectionality. That’s not a disclaimer — it’s a design principle. There is nothing I conceptualize that hasn’t been examined through multiple layers of lived experience, cultural context, and systemic awareness. Nuance isn’t something I add to my work. It’s where my work begins.

“Nuance isn’t something I add to my work. It’s where my work begins.”

My intellectual passions anchor that lens: public health, environmental epidemiology, and education reform. I think about who gets sick and why. I think about which communities bear the weight of environmental neglect. I think about what children are being taught and what they aren’t — and what that costs them. These aren’t academic interests. They are the through lines connecting everything I’ve chosen to spend my time on.

A Creative Eye

I am creative by nature — and I’ve learned that creativity isn’t separate from strategy. It’s what makes strategy land.

I have an instinctive ability to see how media connects to everything: how a story told the right way can shift a policy conversation, how visual identity shapes whether a community program feels trustworthy or foreign, how the framing of an idea determines whether it reaches the people it was built for. I don’t just think about what needs to happen — I think about how it needs to look, feel, and move to actually work in the world.

That creative sensibility shows up in the way I design events, build campaigns, write proposals, and communicate across audiences. It’s not decoration. It’s infrastructure.

A Background in Teaching

Before I coordinated events for civic leaders and built partnerships across Baltimore, I was in classrooms. I have taught children and young people from ages 6 to 18 — from Montessori daycares to charter schools — and that experience gave me a skill set that no office job could replicate.

How to meet people where they are.

A six-year-old and a seventeen-year-old require completely different approaches to the same truth. So does a room full of executives versus a community of first-generation homebuyers. I learned early how to read a room, adapt my communication, and never assume that one size fits anyone.

How to hold a room.

Classroom management is one of the most underrated leadership skills in existence. When you can keep 25 children engaged, curious, and accountable, you can run a meeting, lead a team, and facilitate a conversation with just about anyone.

How to make complexity accessible.

Good teaching is good translation. It requires breaking down systems, ideas, and information into something a person can actually use. I bring that instinct to everything — proposals, presentations, community programming, and strategy.

How to care about outcomes, not just outputs.

Teachers don’t measure success by how much they covered. They measure it by what actually landed. I carry that standard into every project I take on.

Core Values

Nuance over simplicity.

The easy answer is rarely the right one, especially when people are involved.

Process as much as vision.

Ideas without infrastructure don’t change anything. I build the map alongside the dream.

Community as context.

I don’t design for people in the abstract. I design for specific communities with specific histories, specific needs, and specific strengths.

Excellence as a baseline.

Not perfectionism — excellence. There’s a difference. Perfectionism is fear. Excellence is standard.
